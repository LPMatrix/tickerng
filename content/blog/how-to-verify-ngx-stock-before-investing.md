## Step 1: Check Financial Health

**Why it matters:** Strong revenue, earnings, and balance-sheet trends support the story. Weak or worsening numbers often show up here first.

**What to verify:**
- **Revenue and earnings:** Are they growing, stable, or shrinking? Over what period (e.g. 3–5 years)?
- **Profitability:** Net margin, operating margin. Are they positive and stable or improving?
- **Debt:** Total debt vs equity or cash. Can the company service debt from operating cash flow?
- **Cash flow:** Operating cash flow positive and aligned with reported earnings?

**Common mistake:** Focusing only on share price or dividend yield without checking whether earnings and cash flow support them.

**Pro tip:** Compare ratios to sector peers (e.g. other banks, other cement names) so you see relative strength, not just absolute numbers.

---

## Step 2: Look at Valuation

**Why it matters:** Even a good company can be a bad buy if the price is too high. Valuation helps you see whether the market has already priced in the upside.

**What to verify:**
- **P/E (price-to-earnings):** Current and forward if available. How does it compare to the sector and to history?
- **P/B (price-to-book):** Especially relevant for banks and financials.
- **Dividend yield:** Is it sustainable? Check payout ratio (dividends / earnings).
- **EV/EBITDA or similar:** Useful for comparing across capital structures.

**Common mistake:** Using one ratio in isolation. P/E alone can mislead if growth or risk is very different from peers.

**Pro tip:** Note whether the stock is trading above or below its 3–5 year average valuation. That doesn’t tell you to buy or sell, but it tells you if you’re paying a premium or a discount relative to history.

---

## Step 3: List Risk Factors and Growth Catalysts

**Why it matters:** Risks can turn a “good company” into a bad investment. Catalysts can justify paying up or holding.

**What to verify:**
- **Risks:** Regulatory, FX, sector cycle, competition, governance, single-customer or single-product dependence. Which of these could materially hurt earnings or the share price?
- **Catalysts:** New capacity, new products, market share gains, policy or macro tailwinds. What could drive re-rating or earnings upgrades over the next 1–2 years?

**Common mistake:** Ignoring risks because the story sounds good, or ignoring catalysts because the P/E looks high.

**Pro tip:** Write down the top 3 risks and top 3 catalysts. If you can’t articulate them, you haven’t finished your verification.

---

## Step 4: Read the Narrative and Consensus (If Available)

**Why it matters:** Management and analyst views add context. NGX analyst coverage is sparse — use what exists and note when it’s missing.

**What to verify:**
- **Management tone:** Recent reports or calls — are they confident, cautious, or evasive on key issues?
- **Analyst ratings:** Any recent Buy/Hold/Sell or price targets? If none, the audit should say “No recent analyst consensus found” rather than guessing.
- **News:** Regulatory, sector, or company-specific news in the last 6–12 months that could affect the thesis.

**Common mistake:** Treating “no analyst coverage” as a reason to skip verification. It’s a reason to rely more on your own checklist.

---

## Step 5: Form a Verdict (Bull Case vs Bear Case)

**Why it matters:** Verification should end in a clear view: under what conditions would you be right (bull case) and wrong (bear case)?

**What to do:**
- **Bull case:** In 1–2 sentences, what would need to happen for this stock to do well? (e.g. “Earnings grow 15% as new capacity comes on stream and FX stabilises.”)
- **Bear case:** In 1–2 sentences, what would prove you wrong or make you cut the position? (e.g. “Debt refinancing at much higher rates or a sharp naira devaluation.”)
- **Decision:** Given your risk tolerance and portfolio, does the bull case justify the bear case? If yes, you’ve verified enough to consider a position size. If no, pass or wait.

**Common mistake:** Skipping the verdict and “just buying.” Writing it down forces you to be explicit.

**Pro tip:** Revisit the verdict when results or news break. If the bear case is playing out, that’s your cue to reassess.

## Troubleshooting: Common Issues

### “I can’t find recent financials.”
Use the company’s investor relations page, NGX announcements, or a data provider. If data is too old or missing, note it as a limitation and don’t pretend you’ve verified.

### “Valuation looks cheap but the sector is in a downturn.”
Cheap can get cheaper. Factor sector cycle into your risk list and your verdict. Don’t rely on P/E alone.

### “I don’t have time to do this for every idea.”
Use a structured research tool (e.g. EquiScan) to get a first pass: financials, valuation, risks, catalysts, and a bull/bear summary. Then deepen manually for names you’re serious about.
